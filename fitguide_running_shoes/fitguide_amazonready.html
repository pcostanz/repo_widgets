The right running shoes can provide comfort, flexibility, and support during your run and can also contribute to a quick recovery afterward. Finding the ideal amount of cushioning and structure for your stride can minimize excessive friction and prevent blisters.</p> <p>This guide will help you find the right shoe for you. It will explain the basics of running shoe anatomy and what you should look for in regards to how each component of the shoe fits. It will also explain different styles of running and the types of running shoes that are best suited for each style. </p> <p><b>The Four Essential Factors in Choosing and Keeping a Running Shoe Are:</b></p> <div class="af-topnav"> <div class="af-topnav-btn af-topnav-btn-first"><a href="#af-section1">Fit and Feel</a></div> <div class="af-topnav-btn"><a href="#af-section2">Pronation</a></div> <div class="af-topnav-btn"><a href="#af-section3">Heel Drop and Footstrike</a></div> <div class="af-topnav-btn af-topnav-btn-last"><a href="#af-section4">Running Surface and Activity</a></div> <div class="clear"></div> </div> </div> <!-- end header --> <!-- start section 1 --> <div class="container_12" id="af-section1"> <div class="af-spacing"></div> <div class="af-divider"></div> <h2>1. Fit and Feel </h2> <div class="grid_4"> <p style="padding-top: 8px">A running shoe is made up of three basic components: the upper, the midsole, and the outsole. The upper and lining determine how the running shoe feels on your foot while the midsole and outsole combine to cushion and support your stride. Here's a quick look at the components that make up a running shoe and how each should feel on your foot.</p> </div> <div class="grid_8"> <img src="https://images-na.ssl-images-amazon.com/images/G/01/AMAZON_FASHION/2013/SHOES/EDITORIAL/10_FALL_2/Fit_Guide/Fit_Guide_Diagram_alt.jpg"> </div> <div class="clear"></div> <div class="af-spacing"></div> <table class="a-bordered af-centering"> <tbody><tr> <th width="95">Shoe Part</th> <th width="280">Materials</th> <th width="280">How it Should Fit and Feel</th> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">Upper</td> <td>The upper is typically made of mesh and some sort of overlay to provide support.</td> <td>The upper should feel snug and breathable, and should not rub excessively during your stride.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">Midsole</td> <td>The midsole is typically made of some type of foam and can also contain various firmer support elements.</td> <td>The midsole should provide proper arch support, cushion, and rebound. Your foot should feel supported and not slip or flop over the sides.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">Outsole</td> <td>The outsole is typically made of rubber or some durable composite.</td> <td>The outsole should provide adequate traction for your running surface.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">Toe Box</td> <td>The toe box is often covered with a protective overlay to boost durability and reinforcement of support.</td> <td>The toe area should allow room for toes to wiggle while standing. Some runners prefer a wider toe box to allow their toes to splay.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">Heel Counter</td> <td>The heel counter is composed of plastic or other materials used to secure the heel and protect against slippage and abrasion.</td> <td>Your heel can move but not excessively and should be well formed and fit snugly.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">Width Area</td> <td>The upper and the last (the shape of the shoe platform) determine the width.</td> <td> Width should be snug enough that the foot does not slide around easily.</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <div class="clear"></div> </div> <!-- end section 1 --> <!-- start section 2 --> <div class="container_12" id="af-section2"> <div class="af-spacing"></div> <div class="af-divider"></div> <h2>2. <div class="af-divider"></div> <h2>3. Heel Drop and Footstrike </h2> <p>A key measurement of any running shoe is the heel-to-toe differential, or heel drop. This can range anywhere from 0 to 16+ mm. The higher the number, the more thickness and cushion in the heel.</p> <p>Your footstrike determines the amount of heel cushion you need. Most runners strike heel first and require a conventional running shoe to effectively cushion the impact. Other runners choose to tailor their stride to strike at the midfoot or forefoot. Such a stride requires little to no heel cushion.</p> <div class="af-spacing"></div> <table class="a-bordered af-centering"> <tbody><tr> <th width="80">Measurement</th> <th width="290">Heel Drop</th> <th width="280">Footstrike</th> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">0-3 mm</td> <td>Zero-drop or flat shoe to give the barefoot feel.</td> <td>Not recommended for heel-first foot strikers.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">4-8 mm</td> <td>Minimalist shoe with a hint of padding in heel.</td> <td>Not recommended for heel-first foot strikers. Perfect for midfoot or forefoot strikers.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">9-12 mm</td> <td>Average heel drop for running shoes.</td> <td>Perfect for beginner runners and overpronators.</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="af-textcenter">13-16 mm</td> <td>Maximum cushioning for running shoes.</td> <td>Recommended for heel-first strikers and underpronators.</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <div class="clear"></div> </div> <!-- end section 3 --> <!-- start section 4 --> <div class="container_12" id="af-section4"> <div class="af-spacing"></div> <div class="af-divider"></div> <h2>4.
